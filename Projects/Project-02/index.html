Mythological Lyra

Lyra represents the lyre of Orpheus, the legendary musician. The instrument was originally created by Hermes and later given to Orpheus, whose music was so powerful that it could charm gods and nature alike. Orpheus nearly succeeded in bringing his wife Eurydice back from the underworld, but he failed at the last moment. After his death, Zeus placed his lyre in the sky.

Astronomical Lyra

The constellation Lyra is home to Vega, one of the brightest stars in the night sky, located just 25 light-years from Earth. Vega was the North Star around 12,000 BCE and will become the North Star again in about 13,000 years due to precession. Lyra also contains the famous Ring Nebula (M57), a planetary nebula about 2,300 light-years away.

Mythological Orion

Orion was a mighty hunter in Greek mythology who boasted that he could kill all animals. Hera, displeased by his arrogance, sent the Scorpius constellation to defeat him. To prevent further conflict, Zeus placed Orion and Scorpius on opposite sides of the sky, ensuring that they never appear at the same time.

Astronomical Orion

Orion is one of the most recognizable constellations in the winter sky, located near the Milky Way and containing many bright stars. Betelgeuse, a red supergiant about 642 light-years away, is expected to go supernova in the future. Rigel, a hot blue supergiant, is approximately 860 light-years away. The Orion Nebula (M42), a stellar nursery, is about 1,344 light-years from Earth.

Mythological Pegasus

Pegasus was a winged horse born from the blood of Medusa when she was slain by Perseus. Later, the hero Bellerophon tamed Pegasus and used him to defeat the monstrous Chimera. However, Bellerophon tried to ride Pegasus to Mount Olympus, angering Zeus, who caused him to fall. Pegasus, on the other hand, was honored by being placed among the stars.
